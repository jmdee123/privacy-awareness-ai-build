The law, in plain English

What the Privacy Act protects and why it applies to you.

Photo: rawpixel / Unsplash

The Privacy Act 1988

One law. 13 principles.

Governs how personal information is collected, used, stored and shared. Applies to most organisations with turnover over $3M — and many others.

The Act regulates personal information — anything about an identified individual, or someone reasonably identifiable. Names, emails, employee records, health data, financial details, IP addresses in many contexts.

Quick test: If you can connect information back to a specific person — directly or indirectly — it's likely personal information under the Act.

The 13 Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) cover the full lifecycle of personal information:

Open & transparent

Clear privacy policies (APP 1–2)

Collection

Only collect what's needed (APP 3–5)

Use & disclosure

Purpose limitation (APP 6–8)

Accuracy & security

Keep accurate and secure (APP 10–11)

Access & correction

Individual rights (APP 12–13)

These categories require explicit consent and extra care:

Health & medical

Mental health, disabilities, medical history

Race & ethnicity

Racial or ethnic origin

Biometric & genetic

Fingerprints, facial recognition, DNA

Beliefs & affiliations

Political, religious, philosophical

Not everything is personal information. Understanding the difference between these three categories is essential — especially before you decide how to handle something.

🟠 Personal information

Identifies or could identify a specific person. Protected under the Act.

Employee salary
Customer email address
IP address — in most contexts

🔴 Sensitive information

A subset of PI with higher protection. Needs explicit consent. Extra care always required.

Medical diagnosis
Religious belief
Racial or ethnic origin

🔵 Not personal information

Truly anonymised — cannot be linked to any individual. Falls outside the Act's scope.

Anonymised sales totals
Aggregate website traffic
Key rule: Sensitive information is always personal information — but not all personal information is sensitive. When in doubt about which category applies, treat it as the higher protection category until you can check.

Your role as an employee

You make decisions about personal information every single day. That matters.

Photo: Alex Kotliarskyi / Unsplash

What's expected of you

Four things every employee must do.

1

Only collect what's necessary

Ask: do we actually need this? Don't gather data "just in case." Collect only what's relevant. The urge to collect "because it might be useful" is where most unintentional breaches begin.

2

Use it only for its intended purpose

Customer email for invoicing? Don't repurpose it for marketing. Purpose matters — even when the new use seems harmless or beneficial.

3

Keep it secure

Lock your screen. No personal email. No personal cloud. Store files where authorised. The most common breaches in Australian organisations are accidental — wrong recipient, unlocked screen, informal channel.

4

Report when something goes wrong

Suspected breach? Report immediately. The NDB scheme means there's often a legal obligation to act within 30 days — and the clock starts when you become aware, not when it's confirmed.

Making it real

Knowing the rules is step one. Here's how to live them without it becoming a chore.

Photo: Wan San Yip / Unsplash

Privacy in practice

Habits that actually help.

Small consistent actions — that's where privacy actually lives.

Lock your screen whenever you step away — even for two minutes.
Shred or securely dispose of printed documents with personal information.
Be aware of shoulder-surfing in open offices, cafés, and on public transport.
Store files where you're authorised to — not on personal drives, desktops, or USB sticks.
Double-check the recipient before sending any email with personal data. Wrong sends are one of Australia's most reported breach types.
In Teams or Slack, don't share screenshots containing personal data — use secure channels and access controls.
Don't save work data to personal cloud storage without explicit approval.
Check your AI tool policy before pasting any real customer or employee data into any AI system.

Act fast. Don't cover it up.

The NDB scheme means your organisation may need to notify the OAIC and affected individuals within 30 days. The clock starts when you become aware.

Suspect a breach — even a potential one — report to your manager or privacy contact immediately.
Document what happened: what information, who may have seen it, when you discovered it.
Don't try to fix it quietly yourself, and don't discuss externally until your organisation has assessed it.
